# ai201-project4-provenance-guard

# Provenance Guard

A backend API for classifying whether submitted text was written by a human or generated by AI. Built for creative writing platforms to surface attribution context to readers and give creators a path to appeal misclassifications.

---

## Architecture

A submitted piece of text flows through the following components:

1. **POST /submit** validates input and assigns a `content_id`
2. **Signal 1 (LLM)** — sends the text to Groq and gets back a score 0–1
3. **Signal 2 (Stylometric)** — computes sentence length variance and punctuation density in pure Python, returns a score 0–1
4. **Confidence scoring** — combines both signals into a single score (70% LLM, 30% stylometric)
5. **Label generation** — maps the score to one of three plain-language labels
6. **Audit log** — writes a structured JSON entry with all signal scores and metadata
7. **Response** — returns `content_id`, `attribution`, `confidence`, and `label` to the caller

Appeals flow separately: **POST /appeal** looks up the original entry by `content_id`, updates its status to `under_review`, and appends the creator's reasoning to the log.

---

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1 — LLM Classification (Groq)

Sends the text to `llama-3.3-70b-versatile` with a prompt asking it to assess whether the text reads as human or AI-generated. Returns a float 0–1.

**Why it works:** AI models produce text with consistent register, smooth transitions, and generic phrasing. An LLM recognizes these patterns because it produces them itself.

**What it misses:** Formal human writing (academic papers, legal documents) shares surface features with AI output and will score high even when genuinely human-written.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric Heuristics (Pure Python)

Measures two structural properties:
- **Sentence length variance** — standard deviation of word counts across sentences. AI text is more uniform; human writing is more erratic.
- **Punctuation density** — count of expressive punctuation marks (commas, dashes, ellipses, `!`, `?`) per sentence. Humans use these more irregularly.

Both sub-scores are normalized to 0–1 and averaged. High uniformity + low punctuation density → high stylo_score (AI-like).

**What it misses:** Poetry, non-native English speakers writing carefully, and structured prose (numbered lists, reports) all exhibit low natural variance and will score as AI-like even when human-written.

---

## Confidence Scoring

```
combined = (0.70 × llm_score) + (0.30 × stylo_score)
```

The LLM signal carries more weight because it captures semantic and stylistic coherence holistically. Stylometrics provides an independent structural cross-check but is more sensitive to content type.

Thresholds are **asymmetric** — the bar to label something AI-generated is higher than the bar to label it human-written. A false positive (wrongly accusing a human writer) is worse than a false negative on a writing platform.

| Score | Attribution |
|-------|-------------|
| < 0.45 | `likely_human` |
| 0.45 – 0.75 | `uncertain` |
| > 0.75 | `likely_ai` |

### Example submissions

**High-confidence AI (0.7585):**
```
"Artificial intelligence represents a transformative paradigm shift in modern society.
It is important to note that while the benefits of AI are numerous, it is equally
essential to consider the ethical implications. Furthermore, stakeholders across
various sectors must collaborate to ensure responsible deployment."
```
→ `llm_score: 0.80`, `stylo_score: 0.66`, `confidence: 0.7585`, `attribution: likely_ai`

**High-confidence human (0.3014):**
```
"ok so i finally tried that new ramen place downtown and honestly? underwhelming.
the broth was fine but they put WAY too much sodium in it and i was thirsty for
like three hours after."
```
→ `llm_score: 0.20`, `stylo_score: 0.54`, `confidence: 0.3014`, `attribution: likely_human`

## Transparency Label Variants

**High-confidence AI (`confidence > 0.75`)**
> "Attribution unclear — Our automated analysis found patterns consistent with AI-generated text. This is a signal, not a certainty. Use the appeal button below to contest this."

**Uncertain (`confidence 0.45–0.75`)**
> "Attribution uncertain — Our analysis could not determine whether this was written by a person or generated by AI. We've flagged this so you have the context, not to make a judgment."

**Likely human (`confidence < 0.45`)**
> "Appears human-written — Our analysis found no strong indicators of AI generation. This reflects our best assessment, not a guarantee."

## Rate Limiting

`POST /submit` is limited to **10 requests per minute** and **100 requests per day** per IP address.

**Reasoning:** A legitimate creator submitting their own work would rarely submit more than a few pieces per session. 10/minute is generous for real use while blocking automated flooding. 100/day prevents sustained abuse without inconveniencing any real user.

## Known Limitations

**Formal poetry will likely be misclassified.** Poems that use anaphora (deliberate repetition), strict meter, or simple vocabulary produce low sentence length variance and low type-token ratio — both of which the stylometric signal reads as AI-like. A well-crafted sonnet following Shakespearean conventions may score as `uncertain` or even `likely_ai`. The asymmetric threshold partially mitigates this, but it remains a genuine weak spot tied directly to how the stylometric signal is constructed.

## Spec Reflection

**Where the spec helped:** Writing out the three label variants in `planning.md` before building forced a real design decision early. When it came time to implement `get_label()`, the function was trivial to write because the exact strings already existed. Without that step, it would have been easy to write placeholder text and never revisit it.

**Where implementation diverged:** The planning doc laid out a milestone-by-milestone build order — submission endpoint first, then signal 1, then signal 2, then the production layer. In practice, once signal 1 was working I found it easier to implement signal 2 and the confidence scoring logic at the same time rather than treating them as separate steps. The appeals endpoint and audit log also ended up being built together since they share the same log file. The final system matches the spec, but the order of construction was more iterative than the plan suggested.
